Background

Design sprints are an emerging strategy in the product development world. Developed by Google Ventures, they are a fast-paced design thinking process that allows small teams to prototype or explore specific business goals. In this guide, we will explore the details of the process, and share tips on how and why to use them successfully.

Where sprints come from

For the purposes of kicking off this documentation, we are using DockYard’s 4-day sprint schedule as a guide. DockYard’s practice of design sprints is closely based on the Google Ventures 5-day design sprint model, but with a shorter 4-day duration. We also draw on the Agile process and the Scrum methodology to inform our sprints.

Why 4-day sprints
UX Designers are used to working fast and lean. And a four-day schedule leaves room on Fridays to train and explore. Dedicating that day can allow people to remain more focused on project work the other days. One week is also a logical duration for planning into a business schedule. It is neither prohibitively long nor too short, and allows small teams to address significant problems quickly and efficiently, in week-sized chunks.
All of the recommendations in this guide can be applied to a 5-day design sprint schedule. The main difference is the opportunity to split the testing and refining scheduled for Day 4 into two separate days: one for user-testing, and one for making changes based on the test results and potentially testing again to evaluate them. The pace of work you’re comfortable with, as well as your schedule should help determine whether a 4-day or a 5-day Design sprint is best.

A nimble process

The qualities of successful sprints are:

  • Ability to iterate, and do so relatively quickly
  • Taking advantage of a small team working in a focused manner
  • Working together, in person. Everyone can communicate quickly and informally.

Speed and scope

The key difference between a sprint and other methods of working concerns speed and scope. In a sprint, you’re time-boxed to a four (or five) day duration, making you focus on producing actionable insights in that time. The benefit is that you’re able to arrive at a reasonably vetted, considered solution to the problem at hand. However, to allow for success in this short timeframe, you must begin with a narrow enough focus. Speed and scope go hand in hand and you must consider both to succeed.
