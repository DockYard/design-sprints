What’s next

To help illustrate what’s achievable in a sprint, here are some potential outcomes and next steps. Of course, you’ll get different results from a series of several sprints than you’d get from a single one-week effort.

Typical sprint outcomes

A working prototype

For a new product, a one-week design sprint could result in a clickable prototype for one or two simple user stories. In addition, the thinking that went into defining the context of these user stories in days 1 and 2 informed further explorations.

For a more complex product, a four-week sequence of design sprints yielded prototypes of several user flows for two types of users, on two devices: a customer user flow for Mobile, and an Admin user flow for Desktop. With a four-week duration, it was possible to explore the product more deeply and examine the connections and interdependencies between the proposed user flows.

Product Direction

A design sprint can allow the team to take a fresh look at a wide landscape of possibilities and discover and prioritize alternative ways to move forward. When possibilities seem too wide to move forward, a sprint can help select and test out what one of the directions might look like in action.

Typical next steps

After a design sprint, you’ll have clarity, a vetted idea or two, and some test results to base future work cycles on. Here’s what typically happens next:

Engineering and Design

Based on the scope, complexity and optimal solutions proposed in the sprint, you can create a solid proposal for design and engineering work going forward. Running a single sprint early in the process can have a large cost saving effect down the line.

For an Engineering estimate, look out for hidden assumptions that could exponentially increase complexity. How many levels of nesting does a hierarchy contain? How deep is a search? Can you sort by more than one parameter? Do the wireframes show all possible formats of the content, or are there others?

For Design, look at where information is coming from, how content and data will be created, how often things are expected to change, and how variable the form of the content is.

Another sprint!

Because the process is modular, it can be repeated. Sprints can address different aspects of a product separately, or progressively add detail and depth to the results of a previously run sprint.
